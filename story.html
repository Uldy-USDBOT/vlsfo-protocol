Zero Dollars, Zero Code, One Smartphone

By Naji Ali

Chapter 1: The Life That Broke Me

I was 36 years old, and by the standards of the world around me, I was already too late. Too late to fix my life, too late to build something meaningful, too late to become anything more than what I had already become—a taxi driver navigating the streets of Tripoli, carrying strangers while feeling like I had lost direction in my own life.

Every morning felt heavy before it even began. I didn’t wake up because I was rested; I woke up because reality didn’t allow me to stay asleep. The room I lived in wasn’t just quiet—it was empty in a way that reminded me constantly of what I had lost.

My wife had left. She took my son with her. She didn’t leave with anger. She left with exhaustion. That hurt more.

I still remember her last words: “I can’t live like this anymore.”

There was no argument. No shouting. Just truth.

And I had nothing to say back.

I would sit alone for a few minutes every morning before leaving, staring at the wall as if answers might appear. They never did.

Then I would pick up my keys and step back into the same routine—driving.

Tripoli was alive, but not in a hopeful way. The city carried the scars of conflict. People moved, but most were surviving, not living. Conversations in my taxi often revolved around problems, shortages, and frustration.

At the end of the day, after hours of driving, negotiating fares, and dealing with people, I would count my earnings.

Six dollars.

Sometimes less.

Rarely more.

Six dollars was not income—it was survival on delay.

Debt followed me everywhere. It didn’t need to shout. It existed quietly, like a shadow I couldn’t escape.

But despite everything… something inside me refused to die.

While I drove, my mind would drift far away from reality. I imagined businesses, platforms, systems that could exist beyond my current life. I saw solutions to problems I didn’t even have the tools to solve.

And that’s what made it painful.

I could see possibilities… but I couldn’t reach them.

Or at least… that’s what I believed.

Chapter 2: The Idea That Wouldn’t Leave

One day, something small changed everything.

I came across the term VLSFO—Very Low Sulfur Fuel Oil. At first, it was just curiosity. But curiosity quickly turned into focus.

I started searching. Reading. Digging deeper.

And what I found wasn’t just information—it was a problem.

The industry was complex. Information was scattered. Trust was weak. Data was expensive and often hidden behind paywalls.

And that’s when the question appeared in my mind:

What if I solve this?

The idea didn’t make sense.

I had no money. No experience. No access to the industry.

But the idea didn’t leave.

It followed me into my taxi. Into my sleep. Into every moment of silence.

Then one day, something unexpected happened.

Two passengers got into my taxi. They were talking about programming—websites, domains, coding languages.

I didn’t interrupt them. I just listened.

Every word felt important.

When they left, I didn’t charge them.

That conversation was worth more than money.

I went home and started searching.

That’s when I found it.

VLSFO.com

Available.

Price: $12.

Two days of survival.

I didn’t hesitate.

I worked double shifts. I exhausted myself.

Then I paid.

And when I pressed “Buy”… everything changed.

Chapter 3: Pain, Code, and Collapse

I didn’t know how to code.

So I learned.

On a phone.

Every day, every night, typing lines of code on a small screen. Making mistakes. Fixing them. Breaking things. Starting again.

My fingers hurt constantly. My eyes burned from the screen. My mind felt overloaded.

But I didn’t stop.

Until I hit the next wall.

Hosting.

The prices were small for others—but impossible for me.

That night… I broke.

I shouted. I cried. I hated the fact that something so small could stop everything.

I slept, trying to escape.

And I dreamed.

I saw ships. A port. People being misled.

I shouted: “Use my platform!”

They laughed.

I woke up with one decision:

I will not stop.

The next day, an email arrived.

Free hosting.

One month.

I couldn’t believe it.

I kissed my phone.

And started again.

Chapter 4: The First Real Proof

I launched the site.

I told my friends.

They laughed again.

This time, it hurt deeper—but it didn’t stop me.

I connected the site to Google.

Waited.

Nothing.

No visitors.

I shared it everywhere.

One visit.

Just one.

I closed my phone, feeling empty.

Two weeks later… an email arrived.

150 clicks.

My hands started shaking.

This was real.

I wasn’t invisible anymore.

From that moment, everything changed.

I stopped thinking about money.

I started thinking about value.

I built for people I never met.

I researched deeply. I created articles. I shared real data.

I gave everything for free.

I slept hungry… but my mind was full.

Today, I still drive a taxi.

But I am no longer the same man.

Because I proved something to myself:

You don’t need everything to start.

You just need to refuse to stop.
